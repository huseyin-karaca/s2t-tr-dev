\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

\subsection{Background}

Automatic speech recognition (ASR, also referred to as speech-to-text) has progressed significantly with deep sequence models. The Transformer architecture \cite{vaswani2017attention}
 and its convolution-augmented variant, the Conformer \cite{gulati2020conformer}, now achieve state-of-the-art accuracy on benchmarks such as LibriSpeech, outperforming recurrent and CNN models. Self-supervised pretraining \cite{baevski2020wav2vec} further boosts accuracy, and streaming architectures like the RNN-Transducer \cite{Graves2012SequenceTW}, and its Transformer-based extensions enable low-latency transcription. However, these systems are static: they use a fixed model for all inputs. In realistic deployments, acoustic conditions such as noise, channel and speaker can vary unpredictably over time. A static model may be suboptimal across domains or may waste capacity on easy inputs. Consequently, no single ASR system is optimal across all scenarios. Different models excel in different acoustic regimes, forming a complementary set of experts. Leveraging this diversity requires an adaptive mechanism to select the most suitable model for each input, rather than committing to a fixed system. Such adaptive strategies aim to retain the efficiency of single-model inference while benefiting from the diversity of multiple pretrained systems.

A key difficulty in this setting is predicting the performance of
candidate models without explicitly executing them. Observable
properties of the audio signal, such as acoustic statistics, temporal
dynamics, and learned representations, provide useful cues for this
purpose. Nevertheless, many existing approaches summarize an audio
clip into a single pooled descriptor before predicting which expert
to use. This compression discards information that is intrinsic to
speech: each clip is itself a temporal signal whose acoustic
properties evolve from frame to frame.

Within a single clip, the speaker, the channel, the background, and
the spoken content are rarely homogeneous. For example, a single meeting utterance may contain both overlapping speech and clean segments. A parliamentary recording may include near-field and reverberant conditions, while a conversation's speaking rate or emotion may change. The expert that transcribes such a clip most
accurately is typically determined by these intra-clip dynamics,
rather than by any aggregate statistic of the clip.

Neglecting these intra-clip relationships can lead to unstable or
suboptimal routing behavior, particularly in long-form audio where
conditions vary within an utterance. This motivates the development of
adaptive ASR frameworks that explicitly model the frame-level temporal
structure of each clip when routing among experts. In this work, we
follow this direction by formulating ASR model selection as a per-clip
decision problem and introducing a hierarchical transformer router that
operates directly on the frame-level encoder representations of each
clip, rather than on pooled descriptors.

\subsection{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

Modern end-to-end ASR often uses attention-based encoder-decoder or transducer models. The original Transformer eliminated recurrence in sequence-to-sequence tasks and has been widely adapted for speech. In ASR, the Conformer architecture augments self-attention with convolutional modules to better model local speech structure; it achieved near-state-of-the-art LibriSpeech WERs (2.1\% clean, 4.3\% other) while remaining compact. Self-supervised pretraining (e.g. wav2vec 2.0) learns useful speech representations, allowing models to reach very low error rates with limited labels. Streaming extensions include the RNN-Transducer and Transformer-Transducer models (which combine attention with transducer output), as well as chunk-based Conformers for real-time use. These models focus on accuracy and (for streaming ones) low latency, but remain static in that all inputs use the same network.

Latency-Constrained ASR. Streaming variants such as the RNN-T and
Transformer/Conformer transducers (e.g.\ Emformer, Streaming
Transformer-Transducer) optimize the encoder architecture to meet
real-time budgets but do not adapt computation per input. Our work
is orthogonal: instead of changing the inner workings of a single
model, we route a clip to one of several pretrained ASR systems and
decode only that one. The decoding cost therefore matches that of a
single ASR model, while the diversity of the expert pool is
preserved.

Routing and Mixture-of-Experts. Our work is inspired by sparse MoE
and conditional computation. Early Mixture-of-Experts (Shazeer et
al.\ 2017) enabled enormous capacity by routing tokens to only one
expert subnetwork. This idea was scaled in GShard (2020) and Switch
Transformer (2021), where models with hundreds of billions of
parameters were trained with sparsely-gated MoE layers at constant
cost. Beyond NLP, MoE has been applied in vision and other domains
as well. All these models perform intra-network routing: each input
token or frame is dispatched to an expert feedforward layer inside a
single network. In contrast, our router performs inter-model
routing: it selects among independently trained ASR systems at the
clip level. This yields coarse-grained conditional computation that
trades token-level granularity for whole-system specialization and
single-pipeline decoding cost.

There is growing work on adaptive or multi-domain ASR. You et al.\
(2021) proposed SpeechMoE, adding MoE layers to a CTC model with a
learned embedding for the router. SpeechMoE achieved substantial CER
reductions (7--23\% relative) over a static model by dynamically
routing frames to experts. A follow-up SpeechMoE2 (2022) improved
routing by feeding domain- and accent-embeddings to the router.
Hu et al.\ (2023) introduced a Switch-Conformer MoE, a multilingual
streaming Conformer with MoE FFN layers, reporting an $11.9\%$
relative WER drop while activating only $53\%$ of parameters at
inference. Our router differs from these in granularity and scope:
whereas SpeechMoE-style layers route individual frames within one
trained model, we choose among entire pretrained ASR systems for an
entire clip, using the frame-level encoder outputs of those systems
as input to the routing decision.

Conditional Computation and Routing. More broadly, conditional
computation in neural networks includes techniques such as dynamic
skipping of layers (e.g.\ Skip-RNN, BlockDrop), early-exit
classifiers, and adaptive depth. For sequence models, the Routing
Transformer learns sparse attention patterns via content-based
routing, reducing complexity from quadratic to sub-quadratic, and
BASE Layers explore sparse capacity allocation for multi-task
learning. These works route within a single network. In our setting,
the experts are independently pretrained ASR systems with no shared
parameters; the router learns only how to choose among them. This
keeps the experts black-boxed and lets the router benefit from
diverse pretraining without requiring joint optimization.

\subsection{Contributions}

Our contributions are summarized as follows:

\begin{itemize}

\item We introduce an adaptive ASR routing framework that operates on
the frame-level encoder representations of each audio clip, rather
than on a single pooled descriptor. This preserves the intra-clip
temporal structure that informs which expert is best suited for a
given clip and avoids the information loss of pooling-based routers.

\item We formulate adaptive ASR model selection as a per-clip
classification problem over a fixed pool of pretrained ASR experts
and propose a hierarchical transformer router. A per-expert temporal
encoder summarizes each expert's frame sequence with attention-based
pooling, an optional cross-attention bridge lets each summary attend
to the frame-level evidence of the other experts, and a cross-expert
fusion stage produces the final routing decision.

\item We develop a composite training objective that combines a
weighted-WER loss, which directly minimizes the expected transcription
error, with auxiliary cross-entropy terms (hard and temperature-
controlled soft) that strengthen the gradient signal early in
training. The framework supports alternative error metrics without
modifying the routing architecture.

\item Our framework performs input-adaptive ASR model selection using
a lightweight router that consumes encoder hidden states already
produced as a by-product of any ASR pipeline. At inference time, only
the selected expert's full ASR pipeline is decoded, so the decoding
cost matches that of running a single ASR model.

\item The proposed framework is routing-model agnostic. While we
focus on a hierarchical transformer router in this work, the
formulation supports alternative sequence learners, such as recurrent
or convolutional models, without modifying the rest of the pipeline.

\end{itemize}

\section{Problem Description}
\label{sec:problem}

We study adaptive transcription at the level of individual audio clips. Let $\{x_n\}_{n=1}^{N}$ denote a collection of $N$ audio clips, where each clip $x_n$ is itself a temporal signal composed of a sequence of acoustic frames. A clip's frame-level structure captures variations in background noise, speaker identity, accent, speaking rate, and recording quality. State-of-the-art ASR algorithms respond differently to these intra-clip variations \cite{yu2016asrdeep,li2014overviewrobust}, and the best-performing algorithm for a given clip is generally not known in advance.

Let $\mathcal{A} = \{A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_K\}$ denote a finite set of $K$ pretrained ASR algorithms, where $A_j \in \mathcal{A}$ represents the $j$-th algorithm. For each clip $x_n$, the transcription produced by the $j$-th algorithm is
\[
Y_{n,j} = A_j(x_n).
\]
Each transcription $Y_{n,j}$ is compared against the ground-truth reference $R_n$ using a transcription error measure. Using standard metrics such as the Word Error Rate (WER) \cite{li2014overviewrobust}, the transcription loss incurred by using algorithm $A_j$ on clip $x_n$ is
\[
\ell(Y_{n,j}, R_n),
\]
where $\ell(\cdot,\cdot)$ denotes the chosen error metric.

Applying all $K$ algorithms to every incoming clip in order to pick the best transcription is computationally expensive. Instead, the goal is to select a single algorithm $j_n \in \{1,\dots,K\}$ for each clip such that the resulting transcription
\[
\hat{Y}_n = A_{j_n}(x_n)
\]
minimizes the cumulative transcription error across the dataset:
\begin{equation}
\sum_{n=1}^{N} \ell(Y_{n,j_n}, R_n).
\end{equation}

A key challenge is that each clip is internally non-stationary: speaker, channel, and background can shift across frames within the same clip, and these intra-clip dynamics influence which algorithm transcribes the clip most accurately. Selection mechanisms that compress a clip into a single pooled feature vector discard this temporal structure and lose information that is relevant to the choice of expert. The framework introduced in the next section instead operates directly on frame-level encoder representations and uses a transformer-based router to model the temporal structure of each clip when picking an expert.

\subsection{Algorithm Overview}

The proposed framework performs adaptive ASR model selection at the
clip level by exploiting the frame-level temporal structure of each
audio clip. The system operates in two phases: an offline training
phase and an online inference phase, as illustrated in
Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture}. The offline phase learns a
routing model that predicts, from the frame-level encoder
representations of a clip, which expert is most likely to transcribe
that clip with the lowest error. The online phase uses the trained
router to select an expert for each incoming clip and invokes only
that expert to produce the final transcription.

In the offline training phase, the system is provided with a
collection of labeled audio clips $\{(x_n,R_n)\}_{n=1}^{N}$. For each
clip $x_n$ and each expert $A_k\in\mathcal{A}$, the encoder of $A_k$
is run once on $x_n$ to obtain a frame-level hidden-state sequence
\[
\mathbf{H}_n^{(k)}=
\bigl(\mathbf{h}_{n,1}^{(k)},\dots,\mathbf{h}_{n,T_n^{(k)}}^{(k)}\bigr),
\qquad
\mathbf{h}_{n,t}^{(k)}\in\mathbb{R}^{D_k},
\]
where $T_n^{(k)}$ is the number of encoder frames produced by expert
$A_k$ for clip $x_n$ and $D_k$ is its hidden-state dimension. We use
$\mathbf{H}_n^{(k)}$ rather than a pooled summary so that intra-clip
acoustic dynamics are preserved. In parallel, the full ASR pipeline of
each expert is run on the clip to produce its candidate transcription
$Y_{n,k}=A_k(x_n)$ and the corresponding transcription error
$\mathrm{WER}(Y_{n,k},R_n)$. These errors serve as the supervision
signal that tells the router which expert was best for which clip.

The router takes the full set of frame-level hidden-state sequences
$\{\mathbf{H}_n^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}$ as input and produces a probability
vector $\mathbf{w}_n=(w_{n,1},\dots,w_{n,K})$ over the experts. The
parameters of the router are optimized to align $\mathbf{w}_n$ with
the empirical per-clip error vector $(\mathrm{WER}(Y_{n,k},R_n))_{k=1}^{K}$,
using the training objective described in
Section~\ref{subsec:training_objective}.

In the online inference phase, the system receives a new audio clip
$x_n$. Each base encoder is run on $x_n$ to obtain its frame-level
hidden states $\mathbf{H}_n^{(k)}$, which are passed through the
trained router $g_\theta$ to produce $\mathbf{w}_n$. The expert with
the highest predicted probability,
\[
j_n=\arg\max_{k\in\{1,\dots,K\}} w_{n,k},
\]
is selected and only its decoder is executed on the clip to produce
the final transcription $\hat{Y}_n = A_{j_n}(x_n)$. Because only one
full ASR pipeline is decoded per clip, the proposed framework retains
the runtime cost of a single-model system on the decoding side, with
the additional cost of $K$ encoder forward passes and one router
forward pass.

The detailed components of the framework, including the frame-level
feature representation, the hierarchical routing architecture, and
the training procedure, are described in the following subsections.

\begin{algorithm}
\small
\caption{\small Online ASR Model Selection}
\label{alg:online_overview}

\textbf{Inputs:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Audio clip $x_n$
    \item Set of base ASR encoders $\{E_1,\dots,E_K\}$ associated with the experts $\mathcal{A}=\{A_1,\dots,A_K\}$
    \item Trained routing function $g_{\theta}(\cdot)$
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Outputs:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Final transcription $\widehat{Y}_n$ for clip $x_n$
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Inference Steps:}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]

\State For each expert, extract frame-level encoder representations:
\[
\mathbf{H}_n^{(k)} \leftarrow E_k(x_n), \qquad k=1,\dots,K
\]

\State Compute expert selection logits using the routing model:
\[
\mathbf{s}_n \leftarrow g_{\theta}\bigl(\{\mathbf{H}_n^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}\bigr)
\]

\State Normalize logits using softmax to obtain routing probabilities $\mathbf{w}_n$

\State Select expert:
\[
j_n \leftarrow \arg\max_{k \in \{1,\dots,K\}} w_{n,k}
\]

\State Decode the selected expert to obtain the final transcription:
\[
\widehat{Y}_n \leftarrow A_{j_n}(x_n)
\]

\State Return $\widehat{Y}_n$

\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}


\subsection{Frame-Level Feature Representation}
\label{sec:features}

For each clip $x_n$ and each expert $A_k$, we use the encoder of
$A_k$ as a feature extractor and keep its full frame-level
hidden-state sequence
\[
\mathbf{H}_n^{(k)}=
\bigl(\mathbf{h}_{n,1}^{(k)},\dots,\mathbf{h}_{n,T_n^{(k)}}^{(k)}\bigr),
\qquad
\mathbf{h}_{n,t}^{(k)}\in\mathbb{R}^{D_k}.
\]
Different encoders are based on different architectures and frame
rates, so $T_n^{(k)}$ and $D_k$ are model-specific. We do not pool
these frames into a single vector. Instead, the temporal sequence is
exposed directly to the router so that intra-clip dynamics, such as
shifts in noise level, speaker, or speaking rate, can influence the
routing decision.

This choice contrasts with prior model-selection approaches that
compress each clip into a single hand-crafted or pooled feature
vector. While such compact representations are inexpensive, they
discard the temporal structure that often distinguishes the regimes
under which different experts perform well. Keeping frame-level
sequences allows the router to learn, for example, that an expert is
preferable when a clip contains a stretch of overlapping speech or
when its spectral characteristics drift over time.

The frame-level features are obtained as a by-product of the encoder
forward pass that any expert would have to run anyway during
decoding. They therefore introduce no extra acoustic feature
extraction step beyond what the experts already perform.

\subsection{Hierarchical Transformer Router}
\label{subsec:transformer_router}

Given the per-expert frame-level sequences
$\{\mathbf{H}_n^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}$ obtained from the feature pipeline
described in Section~\ref{sec:features}, the routing model has to map
these heterogeneous sequences (different $T_n^{(k)}$ and $D_k$ across
experts) to a single probability vector over the experts. We
implement the router as a hierarchical transformer with two stages.
The first stage produces a per-expert summary that captures the
intra-clip temporal structure as seen through that expert's encoder.
The second stage fuses these summaries across experts to produce the
final selection. An optional cross-attention bridge between the two
stages allows each per-expert summary to attend to the frame-level
representations of the other experts.

\subsubsection{Per-Model Projection and Positional Encoding}

Because the encoder dimension $D_k$ differs across experts, we first
project each frame to a common router dimension $d$ with a
model-specific linear transformation
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{p}_{n,t}^{(k)} = W_p^{(k)} \mathbf{h}_{n,t}^{(k)} + \mathbf{b}_p^{(k)},
\qquad W_p^{(k)} \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times D_k}.
\label{eq:projection}
\end{equation}
A standard sinusoidal positional encoding \cite{vaswani2017attention}
is added to each projected frame to preserve frame ordering within
the clip. To inform the router about which expert produced a given
frame, a learnable model-identity embedding $\mathbf{e}_k\in\mathbb{R}^{d}$
is added to every frame of expert $k$.

\subsubsection{Stage 1: Per-Expert Temporal Encoder}

For each expert $k$ we prepend a learnable summary token
$\mathbf{c}_k\in\mathbb{R}^{d}$ to its projected frame sequence,
forming
\[
\mathbf{X}_n^{(k)} =
\bigl(\mathbf{c}_k,\,\mathbf{p}_{n,1}^{(k)}+\mathbf{e}_k,\,\dots,\,
\mathbf{p}_{n,T_n^{(k)}}^{(k)}+\mathbf{e}_k\bigr).
\]
This sequence is processed by a Transformer encoder
\cite{vaswani2017attention}, denoted by $f_{\text{stage1}}$,
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Y}_n^{(k)} = f_{\text{stage1}}\bigl(\mathbf{X}_n^{(k)}\bigr),
\label{eq:stage1}
\end{equation}
which performs masked multi-head self-attention and a position-wise
feed-forward transformation in each layer. Padding frames are excluded
through a key-padding mask. The output corresponding to the summary
token,
\[
\widetilde{\mathbf{c}}_n^{(k)} = \bigl[\mathbf{Y}_n^{(k)}\bigr]_{0},
\]
serves as a per-expert summary of clip $x_n$ that has been
temporally pooled by attention rather than by averaging.

By default the parameters of $f_{\text{stage1}}$ are shared across the
$K$ experts; this keeps the router compact and lets the model-identity
embedding $\mathbf{e}_k$ carry the expert-specific information. Using
separate Stage~1 parameters per expert is also supported, and is
evaluated as part of the architecture ablation in
Section~\ref{sec:experiments}.

\subsubsection{Cross-Attention Bridge (Optional)}

Stage~1 produces the per-expert summary $\widetilde{\mathbf{c}}_n^{(k)}$
using only the frames of expert $k$. To let each summary be informed
by fine-grained frame-level evidence from the \emph{other} experts,
we optionally insert a cross-attention bridge. For each expert $k$,
let
\[
\mathbf{F}_n^{(\neg k)} = \bigl[\mathbf{Y}_n^{(j)}\bigr]_{1{:}T_n^{(j)}}\,\big|\,j\neq k
\]
denote the concatenated frame-level outputs of all other experts (with
their CLS positions removed). The bridge updates each summary by
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{\mathbf{c}}_n^{(k)} \leftarrow
\mathrm{LN}\Bigl(
\widetilde{\mathbf{c}}_n^{(k)}
+ \mathrm{MHA}\bigl(
\widetilde{\mathbf{c}}_n^{(k)},\,\mathbf{F}_n^{(\neg k)},\,\mathbf{F}_n^{(\neg k)}
\bigr)
\Bigr),
\label{eq:bridge}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{MHA}$ is multi-head attention with the summary as
query and the other-experts' frames as keys and values, and
$\mathrm{LN}$ denotes layer normalization. Because each query is a
single token, this step is inexpensive relative to Stage~1.

\subsubsection{Stage 2: Cross-Expert Fusion}

The (possibly bridged) per-expert summaries
$\bigl\{\widetilde{\mathbf{c}}_n^{(k)}\bigr\}_{k=1}^{K}$ are
concatenated together with a learnable global summary token
$\mathbf{c}^{\star}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}$ to form the Stage~2 input
sequence
\[
\mathbf{Z}_n =
\bigl(\mathbf{c}^{\star},\,
\widetilde{\mathbf{c}}_n^{(1)},\dots,\widetilde{\mathbf{c}}_n^{(K)}\bigr).
\]
A second Transformer encoder $f_{\text{stage2}}$ processes
$\mathbf{Z}_n$ to mix information across experts. The updated global
token, $\widetilde{\mathbf{c}}^{\star}_n$, is fed to a small MLP
classifier to produce the routing logits
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{s}_n =
W_2\,\mathrm{GELU}\bigl(W_1\widetilde{\mathbf{c}}^{\star}_n
+\mathbf{b}_1\bigr) + \mathbf{b}_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{K}.
\label{eq:logits}
\end{equation}
The corresponding routing probabilities are obtained by a softmax
\begin{equation}
w_{n,k} =
\frac{\exp(s_{n,k})}{\sum_{m=1}^{K}\exp(s_{n,m})},
\qquad k=1,\dots,K.
\label{eq:router_softmax}
\end{equation}
The selected expert is the one with the highest predicted
probability, as summarized in Algorithm~\ref{alg:online_overview}.

\subsection{Training Objective}
\label{subsec:training_objective}

The router parameters $\theta$ are learned offline from a labeled
dataset
\[
\mathcal{D}=\{(x_n,R_n)\}_{n=1}^{N}
\]
of audio clips and their reference transcriptions. Before training,
each expert $A_k$ is run on every clip to obtain the candidate
transcription $Y_{n,k}=A_k(x_n)$ and the per-clip Word Error Rate
\[
\mathrm{WER}_{n,k} \;=\; \mathrm{WER}(Y_{n,k},R_n)
\;=\; \frac{S+I+D}{|R_n|},
\]
where $S$, $I$, and $D$ are the substitution, insertion, and deletion
counts from a Levenshtein alignment \cite{li2014overviewrobust}, and
$|R_n|$ is the number of words in the reference. Encoder hidden
states $\{\mathbf{H}_n^{(k)}\}$ are cached at the same time. During
router training, both the WER scores and the encoder features are
treated as fixed inputs.

We combine three loss terms. The first is a primary
\emph{weighted-WER} loss that directly minimizes the expected
transcription error under the router's distribution:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{wer}}(\theta) =
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\sum_{k=1}^{K}
w_{n,k}\,\mathrm{WER}_{n,k}.
\label{eq:loss_wer}
\end{equation}
This loss directly aligns the router with task-level performance, but
the gradient signal that any single sample provides is weak when all
$\mathrm{WER}_{n,k}$ values are similar in magnitude.

To strengthen the supervision early in training, we add an auxiliary
\emph{hard cross-entropy} term against the per-clip oracle expert,
$k_n^\star=\arg\min_{k}\mathrm{WER}_{n,k}$, with label smoothing
$\varepsilon\in[0,1)$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{hard}}(\theta) =
-\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\sum_{k=1}^{K}
\tilde{q}_{n,k}\,\log w_{n,k},
\label{eq:loss_hard}
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{q}_{n,k}=(1-\varepsilon)\mathbb{1}[k=k_n^\star]+\varepsilon/K$.
This term provides a stronger gradient but ignores how much better the
oracle expert is compared to the others.

To preserve the WER-magnitude information while keeping a strong
gradient, we additionally use a \emph{soft cross-entropy} term
against a temperature-controlled target distribution derived from the
WER vector,
\begin{equation}
q_{n,k}^{\mathrm{soft}} =
\frac{\exp(-\mathrm{WER}_{n,k}/\tau)}
{\sum_{m=1}^{K}\exp(-\mathrm{WER}_{n,m}/\tau)},
\label{eq:soft_target}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{soft}}(\theta) =
-\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\sum_{k=1}^{K}
q_{n,k}^{\mathrm{soft}}\,\log w_{n,k}.
\label{eq:loss_soft}
\end{equation}
Small $\tau$ recovers a near-hard target sharply concentrated on the
oracle expert; larger $\tau$ produces a smoother target that reflects
the relative WER gaps between experts.

The overall training objective is a weighted sum
\begin{equation}
\min_{\theta}\;
\mathcal{L}(\theta) =
\lambda_{\text{wer}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{wer}}(\theta)
+\lambda_{\text{hard}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{hard}}(\theta)
+\lambda_{\text{soft}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{soft}}(\theta),
\label{eq:total_objective}
\end{equation}
with non-negative coefficients
$(\lambda_{\text{wer}},\lambda_{\text{hard}},\lambda_{\text{soft}})$
that determine which combination of the three signals is used. The
choice of these coefficients is studied in the loss ablation in
Section~\ref{sec:experiments}.

\vspace{0.1cm}
\noindent\textbf{Remark.}\,\textit{Although WER is used as the
primary error measure due to its widespread use in speech recognition
research~\cite{yu2016asrdeep,li2014overviewrobust}, any non-negative
per-clip error metric, such as Character Error Rate or a
domain-specific score, can be substituted into
\eqref{eq:loss_wer}--\eqref{eq:loss_soft} without modifying the
router architecture.}
\vspace{0.1cm}

The complete offline training procedure is summarized in
Algorithm~\ref{alg:offline_training}.

\begin{algorithm}
\small
\caption{Offline Training of the Hierarchical Router}
\label{alg:offline_training}

\textbf{Inputs}
\begin{itemize}
\item $\{(x_n,R_n)\}_{n=1}^{N}$: Labeled dataset of audio clips and references
\item $\mathcal{A}=\{A_1,\dots,A_K\}$ and their encoders $\{E_1,\dots,E_K\}$
\item Loss coefficients $(\lambda_{\text{wer}},\lambda_{\text{hard}},\lambda_{\text{soft}})$, label smoothing $\varepsilon$, temperature $\tau$
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Output} \\
Trained router $g_{\theta}$

\textbf{Training Steps}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State Split dataset into training and validation sets:
$\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}, \mathcal{D}_{\text{val}}$

\For{$n \gets 1$ \textbf{to} $N$}
    \For{$k \gets 1$ \textbf{to} $K$}
        \State Cache encoder features:
        $\mathbf{H}_n^{(k)} \gets E_k(x_n)$
        \State Decode and score:
        $Y_{n,k} \gets A_k(x_n);\;
        \mathrm{WER}_{n,k}\gets\mathrm{WER}(Y_{n,k},R_n)$
    \EndFor
\EndFor

\For{each training step}
    \State Sample a minibatch $\mathcal{B}\subset\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$
    \State Compute routing probabilities
    $\mathbf{w}_n$ from $\{\mathbf{H}_n^{(k)}\}$ using
    \eqref{eq:projection}--\eqref{eq:router_softmax}
    \State Compute soft target $q^{\mathrm{soft}}_n$ via \eqref{eq:soft_target}
    \State Compute composite loss $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$ on $\mathcal{B}$ via \eqref{eq:total_objective}
    \State Update $\theta$ with a gradient-based optimizer (e.g.\ AdamW)
\EndFor

\State Perform model selection and early stopping using
$\mathcal{L}_{\text{wer}}$ on $\mathcal{D}_{\text{val}}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

This section evaluates the proposed router along three axes. First, a
synthetic experiment isolates the contribution of frame-level
temporal modeling under a controlled regime-switching setting
(Section~\ref{subsec:synthetic}). Second, real-world routing on AMI
and VoxPopuli compares the router against the individual base
models and a set of selection and combination baselines
(Section~\ref{subsec:real}). Third, ablations on the loss and the
router architecture identify which design choices drive the observed
gains (Section~\ref{subsec:ablation}). Section~\ref{subsec:discussion}
discusses what the experiments support and where the framework breaks
down.

Throughout the section we report Word Error Rate ($\mathrm{WER}$) as
the primary metric and, where applicable, the routing-specific
quantities introduced in Section~\ref{sec:workdone}: \emph{selection
accuracy} (the fraction of clips for which the router picks the
oracle expert) and the \emph{oracle gap}
$\mathrm{WER}_{\text{router}}-\mathrm{WER}_{\text{oracle}}$, where
$\mathrm{WER}_{\text{oracle}}$ is the per-clip minimum WER across
experts. The oracle gap measures how far the router is from the
hindsight-optimal selection on the same clips.

\subsection{Synthetic Regime-Switch Experiment}
\label{subsec:synthetic}

\subsubsection{Motivation}

The central design choice in this work is to keep the per-expert
encoder outputs at frame-level resolution rather than collapsing them
into a single pooled vector. A useful sanity check is therefore a
controlled setting in which the optimal expert for a clip is
\emph{determined by the temporal structure of the clip itself} rather
than by any pooled summary of it. If the proposed router exploits
temporal information, it should outperform a router that has access
to the same per-expert features but only after temporal pooling. If
both routers perform similarly, the temporal-modeling claim is not
supported.

\subsubsection{Synthetic Data Generation}

We construct a synthetic dataset of $N=10{,}000$ clips, each with a
fixed length of $T=128$ frames. Each clip $x_n$ is divided into two
halves separated by a regime-switch at frame $\rho_n\sim\mathcal{U}\{T/4,3T/4\}$.
The first half is drawn from one acoustic regime $r_n^{(1)}$ and the
second half from another regime $r_n^{(2)}\neq r_n^{(1)}$; the two
regimes are sampled uniformly from a finite set of $R=4$ regimes.
For each regime we fix a center vector $\boldsymbol{\mu}_r$, and each
frame is generated as
\[
\mathbf{h}_{n,t}^{(k)} =
W_k\bigl(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{r_n^{(t)}}+\boldsymbol{\eta}_{n,t}\bigr),
\qquad
\boldsymbol{\eta}_{n,t}\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\sigma^2 I),
\]
where $W_k$ is an expert-specific projection that simulates the
hidden-state space of expert $A_k$ and $r_n^{(t)}=r_n^{(1)}$ for
$t\le\rho_n$ and $r_n^{(2)}$ otherwise. We use $K=3$ experts.

We assign per-expert WER scores via a fixed table
$\mathrm{WER}_{r_1,r_2,k}$ that depends only on the \emph{ordered
pair} of regimes within the clip. The table is constructed so that
no expert is uniformly best across regime pairs, the best expert
depends on \emph{both} halves (so a router that sees only the
average regime cannot recover it), and a small Gaussian noise is
added to each $\mathrm{WER}_{n,k}$ to make the optimization
non-trivial.

\subsubsection{Models Compared}

We compare four selection strategies on the synthetic data:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Random -- expert chosen uniformly at random per clip.
    \item MLP-pool -- a router that mean-pools each per-expert frame
    sequence to a single vector and feeds the concatenation through
    a 2-layer MLP. Same parameter budget as the proposed router,
    same training loss.
    \item Proposed -- the hierarchical transformer router of
    Section~\ref{subsec:transformer_router}.
    \item Oracle -- selects the per-clip
    $\arg\min_k\mathrm{WER}_{n,k}$.
\end{itemize}
Both learned routers are trained with the same loss
\eqref{eq:total_objective} and the same optimizer, on a $\,80/10/10$
train/validation/test split.

Table~\ref{tab:synthetic} reports the average WER, selection
accuracy, and oracle gap on the test split.
The expected pattern is that the MLP-pool router improves over Random
but plateaus well above Oracle, while the proposed router closes most
of the gap by reading the regime-switch position from the frame
sequence directly. We additionally vary the regime-switch position
$\rho_n$ and the number of regimes $R$ in

advantage scales with the difficulty of the temporal pattern.

\subsection{Real-World Results}
\label{subsec:real}

\subsubsection{Datasets}

We evaluate on two standard long-form English speech corpora:
\begin{itemize}
    \item AMI~\cite{carletta2005ami}: meeting recordings with multiple
    speakers, overlapping speech, and varying microphone conditions.
    We use the official train/dev/test splits and segment the audio
    into utterance-level clips using the provided annotations.
    \item VoxPopuli~\cite{wang2021voxpopuli}: parliamentary speeches
    with diverse speakers, accents, and recording channels. We use
    the English subset and follow the published splits.
\end{itemize}
Both datasets are pre-processed to obtain per-clip references, and
each base ASR model is run once over the entire data to cache its
encoder hidden states and its candidate transcription. The router is
then trained on the cached features.

\subsubsection{Base ASR Models}

We use $K=3$ pretrained ASR models with different backbones:
\begin{itemize}
    \item HuBERT-Large~\cite{hsu2021hubert}, fine-tuned on
    LibriSpeech;
    % (\texttt{facebook/hubert-large-ls960-ft}); 
    encoder dimension $D=1024$.
    \item Whisper-base~\cite{radford2022whisper};
    % (\texttt{openai/whisper-base}); 
    encoder dimension $D=512$.
    \item Wav2Vec2-Large-Robust~\cite{baevski2020wav2vec}, fine-tuned
    on Switchboard;
    % (\texttt{facebook/wav2vec2-large-robust-ft-swbd-300h}); 
    encoder
    dimension $D=1024$.
\end{itemize}
These three models are trained on different data and have different
strengths; AMI and VoxPopuli are out-of-distribution for at least one
of them, which is the regime where adaptive selection is expected to
help.

\subsubsection{Baselines}

We compare the proposed router against:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Single base model (HuBERT, Whisper, W2V2 separately): no
    selection, runs the same model on every clip.
    \item Random: picks an expert uniformly at random per clip.
    \item Weighted random: samples an expert from the prior
    proportional to inverse training-set WER.
    \item ROVER~\cite{fiscus1997rover}: hypothesis-level voting that
    runs all $K$ experts and combines their outputs.
    \item Weighted ROVER: ROVER with per-expert confidence weights
    set from training-set WER.
    \item MLP-pool selector: same as in
    Section~\ref{subsec:synthetic}; mean-pools each expert's
    frame-level features and feeds the concatenation to an MLP
    classifier, trained with the same loss as the proposed router.
    \item Oracle: per-clip $\arg\min_k\mathrm{WER}_{n,k}$, an upper
    bound on any selector that picks one of the $K$ experts.
\end{itemize}
The single-model and ROVER baselines bound what can be achieved
without learning to route, and the MLP-pool baseline isolates the
contribution of the frame-level transformer relative to a purely
clip-pooled learner.

\subsubsection{Training Setup}

The router is trained with AdamW (learning rate $10^{-4}$, weight
decay $10^{-2}$), linear warmup of $200$ steps followed by cosine
annealing, batch size $8$, gradient clipping at $1.0$, and mixed
precision. The default hyperparameters are $d=256$, $n_{\text{heads}}=4$,
$\ell_{\text{stage1}}=2$, $\ell_{\text{stage2}}=1$,
$d_{\text{ffn}}=512$, dropout $0.15$. Frame sequences longer than
$T_{\max}=2000$ are truncated. Cross-attention bridge is enabled and
Stage~1 weights are shared across experts unless stated otherwise.
For the main results we use the best-performing loss configuration
identified in Section~\ref{subsec:ablation}, namely
$(\lambda_{\text{wer}},\lambda_{\text{hard}},\lambda_{\text{soft}})=(1,0,0.5)$
with soft-CE temperature $\tau=0.1$. Early stopping uses
$\mathrm{WER}_{\text{router}}$ on validation with patience $10$.

Two patterns are worth noting. First, selection accuracy and final
WER are not aligned: \emph{Hard CE only} and \emph{WER $+$ Hard CE}

WER, because picking the oracle expert is rewarded equally
regardless of how much better the oracle was. Second, the
\emph{WER $+$ Soft CE} combination achieves both the lowest WER and
the smallest oracle gap, despite a moderate selection accuracy: the
soft target carries WER-magnitude information into the gradient,
which steers the router toward decisions whose mistakes are cheap.
This is the configuration we report as the default in
Section~\ref{subsec:real}.

\subsection{Discussion}
\label{subsec:discussion}

The synthetic experiment isolates the temporal-modeling claim and
shows that frame-level routing outperforms a same-budget pooled
router when the regime-switch is the only source of expert-selection
information. The real-world experiments translate this into
end-to-end WER reductions on AMI and VoxPopuli relative to the best
single base model and the ROVER and MLP-pool baselines, while only
running one full ASR pipeline per clip at inference time.

The loss ablation surfaces a non-obvious point: optimizing the
selection accuracy and optimizing the resulting WER are not the
same objective. The soft cross-entropy term, by carrying the WER
gaps directly into the gradient, drives the router toward decisions
whose worst case is bounded, even when it sometimes \emph{misses}
the oracle expert in cases where the gap is small. This matches the
intuition that what matters operationally is how bad the chosen
expert is, not how often it equals the oracle.

The proposed framework has two limitations worth stating. First, the
router still requires the encoder of every base model to run on each
clip, even though only one full ASR pipeline is decoded; the savings
come from the decoder side. For models with comparable encoder and
decoder costs, the inference saving is correspondingly smaller.
Second, the router operates per clip and does not share information
across clips of the same recording. Long-form scenarios where speaker
and channel persist across clips are likely to benefit from
across-clip context, which we leave as a natural extension.
